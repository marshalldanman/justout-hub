Emergency Response Steps

1Call your carrier immediately (AT&T: 611, T-Mobile: 611, Verizon: *611) — tell them you suspect a SIM swap and request an immediate freeze on your account.
2Change your Apple ID password from a trusted device (not the compromised phone). Go to appleid.apple.com from a computer.
3Change passwords on all financial accounts (bank, crypto, PayPal, Venmo) — start with the highest-value accounts first.
4Switch all accounts from SMS-based 2FA to an authenticator app (Google Authenticator, Authy, or Apple's built-in Passwords app).
5Contact your bank and credit card companies — request a fraud alert and temporary account freeze.
6File an identity theft report at identitytheft.gov and a police report with your local department.
7Place a credit freeze at all three bureaus: Equifax (1-800-685-1111), Experian (1-888-397-3742), TransUnion (1-888-909-8872).

Prevention — Lock Down Your iPhone Number

1Set a carrier PIN/passcode with your provider. AT&T: "Extra Security" in myAT&T app. T-Mobile: call 611 to set account PIN. Verizon: set "Account PIN" in My Verizon app.
2Enable BOTH a SIM lock AND a port-out lock. A SIM lock blocks SIM/eSIM changes on your current carrier. A port-out lock prevents your number from being transferred to another carrier. Attackers use whichever route is easier — you need both. T-Mobile: "Account Takeover Protection" in app. AT&T: "Number Transfer Freeze."
3iPhone SIM PIN (Settings > Cellular > SIM PIN) only protects against physical SIM theft — it does NOT prevent remote SIM swap fraud. Set it anyway (default is 1111), but know that your real protection is at the carrier level.
4Convert to eSIM if possible (Settings > Cellular > Convert to eSIM). eSIMs prevent physical theft, but are NOT immune to remote SIM swaps — a hacker can still convince your carrier to issue a new eSIM. Note: T-Mobile's "SIM Protection" does not cover eSIM transfers on iPhones.
5Replace SMS 2FA with phishing-resistant methods everywhere. Best: hardware security keys (YubiKey) or FIDO2 passkeys (immune to SIM swap AND phishing). Good: authenticator apps (Apple Passwords app, Google Authenticator). Worst: SMS-based 2FA — avoid for all important accounts.
6Enable Apple's Advanced Data Protection: Settings > [Your Name] > iCloud > Advanced Data Protection. This adds end-to-end encryption to your iCloud data. Also review Settings > [Your Name] > Sign-In & Security to audit your trusted phone numbers.
7Secure your carrier account's online portal separately — use a unique strong password and app-based 2FA for your carrier login. Some attackers skip phone calls entirely and do the SIM swap through the carrier's website using stolen credentials.

How SIM Swapping Works

What is a SIM Swap Attack?

A SIM swap (also called SIM hijacking or SIM porting) happens when a hacker convinces your mobile carrier to transfer your phone number to a SIM card they control. Once they have your number, they receive all your calls, texts, and — critically — SMS verification codes. This lets them bypass 2FA on your bank, email, crypto, and social media accounts.

How Hackers Do It

Social engineering: They call your carrier pretending to be you, using personal info gathered from data breaches, social media, or the dark web. They claim they "lost their phone" or "got a new SIM." Some hackers bribe carrier store employees directly — a T-Mobile insider was caught selling SIM swaps for $100 each. In March 2025, a California arbitrator ordered T-Mobile to pay $33 million after attackers bypassed their "NOPORT" security flag by convincing a call center agent to issue a remote eSIM QR code. Others use phishing to steal your carrier login credentials and do the swap through the carrier's website. Some attackers also barrage victims with calls or texts to make them turn off their phone or ignore real carrier alerts.

iPhone-Specific Risks & The eSIM Reality

eSIM-only iPhones (US iPhone 14+) prevent physical SIM theft, but are NOT immune to remote SIM swaps. The vulnerability is the carrier's authentication process, not the type of SIM. If a hacker convinces your carrier to issue a new eSIM, your embedded eSIM gets deactivated remotely. T-Mobile has confirmed their "SIM Protection" feature does not cover eSIM transfers on Apple iPhones. Your carrier controls your phone number and the SIM/eSIM that proves "this is your line" — your phone can be locked down like Fort Knox and you can still be SIM swapped if your carrier account is easy to hijack. Check Settings > [Your Name] > Sign-In & Security to review your trusted phone numbers.

How Common Is This? (2024-2026 Data)

The FBI reported nearly $26 million lost to SIM swap attacks in 2024 alone. The UK's 2025 Fraudscape report reveals a 1,055% surge in SIM swap cases — from 289 in 2023 to nearly 3,000 in 2024. High-profile victims include Jack Dorsey (Twitter CEO's account hijacked), crypto investors (one lost $24 million, another $38 million via T-Mobile breach), and journalists. You don't need to be famous to be targeted — automated tools now let attackers attempt SIM swaps at scale, and the attack is the fastest-growing cybersecurity concern of 2026.
